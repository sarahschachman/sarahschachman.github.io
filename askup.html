AskUp

Overview

September 2014 - now

AskUp is an educational tool to help you make sense and remember material you learn in class, at home, or in the hospital. It uses evidence-based principles to improve your learning. As it turns out, students don't actually know how to study very well -- skimming over notes and watching lecture videos are not very good methods for retaining information. Self-testing has been shown to be one of the most effective learning strategies. Generating questions will require you to recall information, identify the main points, and reflect on how it relates to things youâ€™ve previously learned. Educational strategies such as spaced repetition, question generation, and testing have been proven to have lasting effects on retention, and AskUp intends to make all of these strategies easily applicable to students while studying for courses. AskUp is a joint venture between investigators at Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center (BIDMC) and engineers in the Teaching and Learning Technologies (TLT) program at Harvard University -- where I work. Our project was awarded a Spark Grant from the Harvard Initiative for Teaching & Learning (HILT).

Deliverable

AskUp is a Ruby on Rails app that I coded from scratch beginning in the fall of 2014. When I left to study abroad, it was taken over by two other student developers, and now that I'm back I am continuing to work on it, this time focusing on the front-end and UX. We aim to have V1 ready to be tested in Harvard classes at both the Med School and College by the end of the semester.

How it works

The core of AskUp revolves around the idea of synthesizing what you've learned by generating questions, and participating in discussion surrounding those questions. A student interested in using AskUp to help them study for a certain class will be able to sign up and begin generating questions pertaining to that class. This is not Piazza, however; AskUp is more akin to a crowd-sourced study guide. When a student generates a question for a class, they must reflect on a particular idea they have learned, synthesize the takeaways, and give the relevant answer. Students are also able to quiz themselves on questions generated by other students. When looking at the list of questions in a certain class, a student can quickly quiz themselves. After they answer and the correct answer is shown, they can decide if they got it wrong, right, or somewhere in between. This eventually will tie into our spaced repetition algorithm, so that questions they don't know well will come back to them again. Additionally, students can view a question's thread, which consists of other students' answers and constructive comments about the question and answers.

What we're working on currently

There's still a few things that need to be done technically before AskUp can be launched for testing in classes:

  • Onboarding and modules that help teach the users how to generate good questions. This is perhaps the most important next step, because generating powerful and thoughtful open-ended questions is the core of AskUp. We want to create a memorable way to teach students about what makes a thoughtful question, and to keep this at the forefront of their minds when they create future questions.
  • Integration with Canvas. At Harvard, all of our classes are based online in Canvas. We want to integrate AskUp with Canvas classes, so that students can easily access the platform from their Canvas dashboard.
  • Creation of "institutions" -- we want to offer AskUp at many different schools, but currently it only has a concept of one overarching institution. This means that any classes we add from outside Harvard will be lost in the fray. We would like users to have the ability to choose their institution upon signup, so that they can see classes relevant to them immediately.
  • Commenting and upvoting on questions and answers. Feedback is critical in learning to formulate better questions and add throughtful comments to the discussion, so we think comments and upvotes reminiscent of Stack Overflow will be a powerful addition to the platform.
  • Spaced reptition for questions. This means that when a student says they answered a question well, it will come back less often than a question that they continue to miss. Spaced reptition is one of the main ideas of effective studying, but this will require a lot of thought regarding the underlying algorithms.

UX Research & Design

Conspyr

Conspyr with friends: casual planning made easy.

Overview

Spring 2014

Conspyr is designed for you to see what everyone is doing within your circle of acquaintances. It makes it easy to find friends who are available for lunch, going to the gym, doing small errands, or any other tasks that might be hard for people to find friends to partner up with. The product is designed to only show people who are readily available during the day, so that people who are preoccupied with class or a job during the day arenâ€™t bothered with annoying requests or updates. Conspyr also allows you to let your friends know you want to be joined for an activity, and notifies you when they agree to come along. Conspyr will help open up opportunities to spend time with people you may never have reached out to otherwise.

The Problem

We wanted to solve a problem that we saw prevalent on campus: people's phones blowing up because someone on a group text wants someone to eat lunch with, or go to the gym with, etc. However a lot of the time this becomes a nuisance. It's fantastic when you're down and you get to hang out with a friend, but if you're in a meeting, you don't want to 30 texts about everyone's plans to meet up for lunch. Conspyr aims to solve this problem by giving people a super-simple platform to create super-casual events with temporal chats, so your group text only includes relevant people at the relevant time.

Deliverable

Conspyr was created for the class CS 179: Design of Usable Interactive Systems. With a group of 3 other people, we learned the entire design process, from initial brainstorm to final product, while continually incorporating user feedback. It's actually still live at _____________.

How it works

Conspyr's UI is minimalist and easy to use. All you do is set your name in the settings, and it will remember you locally. From here, you can either browse events that your friends have sent you, or create one yourself. Events won't bother you with notifications, since that is one of the issues we were trying to move away from. Instead, you are free to browse events whenever you're free, to see if someone is also looking for a lunch or gym buddy. When you click on an event, you are taken to the event's page which is simply a chat. This is a convenient way to chat with involved friends and coordinate details all within the context of the event. No more one-off group texts! If you want to create an event yourself, you simply need to specify the date, location and title. Now, you are taken to a list of all of your friends using the app -- you can send the invite to whoever you want to be in on the plans. Eventually we wanted a way to have groups of friends for easy selection (e.g. "Roommates," "CS Study Group," "Bball team," etc.).

User research

One of the main takeaways from this class was the importance of user testing and incorporating their feedback into new iterations of your prototype. We went through many prototypes of various fidelities. We spoke to a huge range of people, from students studying in our dining halls, to tourists in the yard, and we even got feedback from Amazon Turk. This diverse feedback helped us scope out the market, and helped us refine our interactions.
